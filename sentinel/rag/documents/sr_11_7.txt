SR 11-7: Guidance on Model Risk Management
Federal Reserve Board and OCC — April 4, 2011

SECTION 1: PURPOSE AND SCOPE

This guidance addresses supervisory expectations for a sound model risk management framework.
It applies to all financial institutions and covers all models used for decision-making,
particularly those used in consumer-facing financial services.

SECTION 2: DEFINITION OF MODEL

A model is defined as a quantitative method, system, or approach that applies statistical,
economic, financial, or mathematical theories, techniques, and assumptions to process
input data into quantitative estimates. Models are used to identify, measure, monitor,
and control risk exposure.

SECTION 3: MODEL RISK

Model risk occurs primarily for two reasons:
1. A model may have fundamental errors and produce inaccurate outputs when viewed against
   the design objective and intended business uses.
2. A model may be used incorrectly or inappropriately.

SECTION 4: MODEL DEVELOPMENT, IMPLEMENTATION, AND USE

4.1 Documentation Requirements
Banks should have documentation sufficient to allow parties unfamiliar with a model to
understand its overall structure and theory, the data used to develop and test the model,
the mathematical calculations performed, the assumptions relied upon, the limitations
identified, and the circumstances under which the model may not perform well.

Documentation should include:
- Purpose and intended use of the model
- Theoretical basis and mathematical framework
- Data sources, transformations, and quality controls
- Validation methodology and results
- Key assumptions and their justification
- Known limitations and compensating controls
- Approval chain and governance records
- Model performance benchmarks and acceptance criteria

4.2 Model Validation Requirements
Model validation is the set of processes and activities intended to verify that models
are performing as expected, in line with their design objectives and business uses.
Effective validation addresses three core elements:
(a) Evaluation of conceptual soundness
(b) Ongoing monitoring
(c) Outcomes analysis

4.3 Validation Independence
Model validation should be conducted by staff who are independent of the model
development process. The validation function should have appropriate authority to
challenge model developers and users.

SECTION 5: GOVERNANCE, POLICIES AND CONTROLS

5.1 Board and Senior Management Oversight
Banks should have board-level oversight of model risk, with senior management responsible
for ensuring model risk is appropriately managed. The board should understand the
institution's model risk exposure and approve policies for model risk management.

5.2 Model Inventory
Banks should maintain a comprehensive inventory of models in use, including:
- Model purpose and business use
- Risk tier classification (I through IV based on materiality and consumer impact)
- Validation status
- Owner and primary contact
- Date of last validation
- Known limitations

5.3 Risk Tiering
Models should be classified by risk tier:
- Tier I: Low risk — internal operational models, limited financial impact
- Tier II: Moderate risk — consumer-facing matching and recommendation models
- Tier III: High risk — agentic AI systems, autonomous decision-making
- Tier IV: Critical risk — systemic risk models, capital calculation models

SECTION 6: VENDOR AND THIRD-PARTY MODELS

Banks that use vendor models should apply the same rigor of validation and oversight
as internally developed models. The bank remains fully responsible for model risk
management regardless of model source.

SECTION 7: AI AND MACHINE LEARNING MODELS

7.1 Applicability
This guidance applies fully to AI/ML models including:
- Supervised learning models for classification and regression
- Unsupervised learning models for clustering and anomaly detection
- Natural language processing models for document analysis
- Generative AI and large language models used in advisory contexts
- RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) systems

7.2 Special Considerations for AI/ML
- Explainability: AI/ML models must be capable of providing explanations for their
  outputs in terms understandable to the affected consumer
- Fairness: Models used in consumer-facing decisions must be evaluated for disparate
  impact across protected classes
- Adversarial robustness: Models should be tested against adversarial inputs
- Concept drift: Ongoing monitoring must include detection of model drift

7.3 Documentation for AI/ML Models
In addition to standard documentation requirements, AI/ML models require:
- Training data description including demographic composition
- Fairness evaluation methodology and results
- Explainability approach (e.g., SHAP, LIME, attention weights)
- Red team / adversarial testing results
- Drift monitoring approach

SECTION 8: CONSUMER PROTECTION REQUIREMENTS

For models that affect consumer financial decisions:
- Results must be explainable to the affected consumer
- Adverse action notices must include specific, accurate reasons
- Disparate impact must be regularly evaluated and documented
- FCRA / Equal Credit Opportunity Act requirements apply

SECTION 9: ONGOING MONITORING

9.1 Frequency
- High-risk models (Tier III and IV): monthly or upon significant market events
- Moderate-risk models (Tier II): quarterly
- Low-risk models (Tier I): semi-annually

9.2 Triggers for Off-Cycle Validation
- Significant change in model performance metrics
- Material change in model inputs or outputs
- Changes in the business environment affecting model assumptions
- Discovery of model errors or material limitations
- Regulatory or legal changes affecting model use

9.3 Performance Benchmarks
For fairness monitoring:
- Demographic Parity Ratio must remain above 0.80
- Equalized Odds Difference must remain below 0.10
- Disparate Impact Ratio must remain above 0.80
